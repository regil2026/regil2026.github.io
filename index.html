Abstract

Learning visuomotor manipulation policies from a single demonstration is challenging due to severe distribution shift and the costly online interaction. In this work, we propose ReGIL, a retrieval-guided imitation learning framework that enables robots to learn effective manipulation skills from only one demonstration. Our key insight is to treat the demonstration as a reusable non-parametric memory that can guide online learning rather than just the static supervision. ReGIL combines demonstration retrieval with policy optimization to provide three complementary signals: exploration guidance, reward shaping, and policy regularization. Specifically, the demonstration serves as a queryable external memory for exploration and reward shaping. Successful trajectories collected during the retrieval-replay phase provide the regularization distribution for policy optimization, enabling stable online improvement from limited data. We evaluate ReGIL on challenging robotic manipulation tasks from the LIBERO and Meta-World benchmarks under the single demonstration setting. Experimental results show that ReGIL outperforms prior imitation and reinforcement learning baselines in terms of success rate and training efficiency. In real-robot experiments, using only one demonstration and less than one hour of online training, ReGIL achieves over 80% success rate across three manipulation tasks. These results demonstrate that ReGIL is a practical and effective method for data-efficient robot skill acquisition.

Method

The left illustrates the retrieve and replay exploration. A Vision Foundation Model (VFM) extracts visual features from both current trajectories and expert demonstrations to retrieve similar expert segments. These segments are leveraged for action replay and similarity-based reward shaping, yielding the reward signal r. The right panel shows the Online Optimization stage, where the policy is optimized through environment interaction using the retrieval-based reward along with a decaying imitation regularization term.
